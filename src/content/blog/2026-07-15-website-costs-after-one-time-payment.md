When I tell business owners my pricing is [one-time — no subscription, no monthly fee](/blog/how-much-does-a-custom-website-cost-in-india) — I can see the silent question forming: *"Okay… but what will I* actually *pay every month? There's always something."*

It's the right question. The market has trained you to ask it, because almost everyone in this industry hides a recurring charge somewhere. So here is the complete list — the real recurring costs, the optional ones, and the fake ones you're probably paying today.

## The two real recurring costs

**1. Your domain: ~₹800–1,500 per year.** The name itself — `yourbusiness.com` — is rented annually from a registrar (GoDaddy, Namecheap, Hostinger). Nobody on earth can sell you a domain "forever"; a lifetime-domain offer is a lie by definition. Some fancy extensions cost more, but a normal `.com`/`.in` sits in this range.

**2. Hosting: ₹0 for most small-business websites — genuinely.** The websites I build run on platforms like Netlify with backends on services like Supabase, whose free tiers comfortably serve a typical business site's traffic. This isn't a trial or a trick; it's how modern infrastructure is priced — you pay when you're big, and by the time you're big enough to pay, hosting bills are a rounding error next to the business it brings you. A high-traffic platform or heavy storage needs (thousands of photos, video) can push you into paid tiers — usually a few hundred rupees a month, and I'll tell you *before* the build if your project is heading there.

**That's the whole list.** Domain yearly, hosting mostly zero. Everything below is either optional or shouldn't exist.

## The optional costs — real, but only if you want them

- **Professional email** (`you@yourbusiness.com`): free via Zoho Mail's basic plan, or ~₹160/user/month on Google Workspace if you live in Gmail. Optional but recommended — invoices from a Gmail address cost you trust.
- **Payment gateway fees:** if your site takes payments, the gateway takes its ~2% per transaction. That's a cost of *selling*, not of the website — you'd pay it on a payment link or card machine too. ([Links vs integration, explained honestly](/blog/razorpay-payment-links-vs-gateway-integration).)
- **Third-party services your business chooses:** SMS/OTP credits, a live data feed, a booking engine. These exist only if your website *does* something that consumes them — and they're billed by the provider to *your* account, at cost, transparently.

## The fake costs — what the market bills you anyway

Now the commentary. Here's what I keep seeing on invoices when business owners show me what they currently pay:

**The "maintenance" retainer: ₹500–₹3,000/month for nothing.** For a static business website, there is no monthly maintenance. Nothing rusts. No oil change. The honest version of maintenance is *"you'll occasionally want changes, pay per change or keep a retainer if changes are frequent"* — a choice, agreed upfront. The dishonest version is a mandatory monthly fee for an activity that does not exist. Ask your agency exactly what they did last month. Specifically. Watch what happens.

**The hosting markup.** Agencies commonly resell ~₹100/month hosting at ₹500–1,000/month, bundled vaguely into that maintenance fee. You can't see it because the invoice says "hosting & maintenance" as one line. Unbundle it.

**The domain hostage situation — the worst one.** The agency registers *your* domain under *their* registrar account. Everything's fine until you try to leave: suddenly the renewal is pricey, transfers are slow, emails go unanswered — because they're holding your business's name, and they know it. I've seen owners pay years of inflated fees rather than fight for their own domain. **The fix is simple and non-negotiable: the domain lives in an account with your email, your password, your payment method.** A developer who resists that is telling you their retention strategy.

**The panic renewal.** A cousin of the hostage trick: the site "needs renewal" annually — some vague ₹3,000–5,000 to "keep it live," priced as one urgent, unitemised number a week before it "expires." What actually expires is the ₹900 domain.

## The ownership checklist

Whether you work with me or anyone else, "you own your website" should mean, concretely:

1. **Domain** — registered in an account you control, your email on file
2. **Hosting** — account in your name, even when the plan is free
3. **Code** — you have it (a repository handover), so any developer can work on it
4. **Payment gateway** — your business's account, settling to your bank
5. **Every password** — written down, in your hands, on day one

If you can tick all five, no one can ever hold your website against you — including me. That's the actual product I'm selling when I say one-time payment: not just a website, but the absence of a landlord.

## Frequently asked questions

### What are the unavoidable recurring costs of a website in India?

The domain (~₹800–1,500/year) and hosting — which, for most small-business websites built on modern platforms, is genuinely ₹0 on free tiers. Everything else is either optional (email, payment fees) or padding.

### Is monthly website maintenance necessary?

For a typical business website: no. Content changes are per-task work you request when you need it. A mandatory monthly maintenance fee on a site that isn't changing is the industry's most common padding charge.

### My agency pays for my domain. Is that a problem?

It's fine only if the domain is registered in an account *you* control. If it's in the agency's account, you're one dispute away from losing your business's name. Ask for a transfer to your own registrar account — a cooperative agency will do it within days.

### Why do you build on free hosting tiers? Is it reliable?

Platforms like Netlify serve enormous production traffic; their free tiers are a pricing decision, not a quality tier. The account is opened in your name, so there's no dependency on me — and if your traffic ever outgrows it, you upgrade the same account with a card.

### What happens if I need changes a year after launch?

You ask, we agree a price, I make them — or any developer of your choice does, because you have the code. That's the point of ownership: future changes are a *choice with a market*, not an obligation to one vendor.
